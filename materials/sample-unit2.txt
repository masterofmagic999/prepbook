The Silk Road was a network of trade routes that connected China, Central Asia, and the Mediterranean world from around 200 BCE to 1450 CE.

Merchants traveled these routes exchanging goods like silk, spices, glassware, and precious metals. Beyond material goods, the Silk Road facilitated the spread of religions including Buddhism, Islam, and Christianity, as well as technological innovations like papermaking and gunpowder.

The Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE) was particularly important for Silk Road trade, as China was politically stable and actively encouraged commerce. Chang'an, the Tang capital, became a cosmopolitan city with traders, diplomats, and monks from across Eurasia.

The Black Death spread along these same trade routes in the 14th century, demonstrating how networks of exchange could carry both beneficial and harmful elements across vast distances.
